How does real science actually get done?
Help us record it.

We are building open datasets of scientific work — from single computational tasks to entire multi-month research projects — so that AI agents can learn to understand, assist, and eventually carry out research. Three complementary efforts are recruiting participants and collaborators right now.

Across UMN: Chemistry · Biology · Medicine · Aerospace · Civil & Environmental · Materials Science · Electrical & Computer Eng. · Robotics · & more Three time horizons: tasks → sessions → projects · Open data, co-authorship for contributing labs
Now recruiting graduate students, lab members, and PIs across departments — refer an interested colleague or contribute your own lab's work.

One question, three scales of data

Each project captures scientific work at a different granularity. Together they span the full arc of research — what a scientist does in an hour, and what unfolds over half a year. They share a participant pool and recording infrastructure across departments.

1–2 hour simulation & analysis tasks → SciHarbor
Short · hands-on
1–2 hour physical lab sessions → Lab Workflows

SciHarbor

Lead: Young-Jun Lee · 1–2 hr per task

If your lab works with simulators or computational tools: contribute well-specified scientific tasks that become a public benchmark for AI agents on real scientific software.

  • CapturesTask spec, expected outcome, evaluation metrics
  • MethodInterview + structured task submission (YAML manifest)
  • BecomesSci-MCP server + evaluation harness + live leaderboard
  • Phase 2Physical workflows once robotic arms are installed

Contact: Young-Jun Lee ↗

SciHarbor — Project Brief flyer in progress
  • A benchmark platform evaluating AI agents on real scientific simulators — OpenFOAM, GROMACS, AlphaFold, Ansys, SAP2000, OpenRocket, and more.
  • 300+ tasks in Phase 1; each participating lab contributes ~20 expert-validated tasks and owns the ground truth.
  • TerminalBench-style public leaderboard, MIT-licensed, results and traces open by default.
  • Supported by the Laude Slingshot Grant 2026.

Lab Workflows

Lead: Karin de Langis · 1–2 hr per session

If your lab is running physical experiments: record short lab sessions with a head-mounted camera and think-aloud narration so models can learn the goals behind each action.

  • CapturesHead-mounted video + think-aloud, annotated intents
  • MethodWear a camera, record, narrate your work
  • BecomesBenchmark of how actions connect to scientific goals
  • Commitment~3 hours total, across three steps

Contact: Karin de Langis ↗

If your lab is running physical experiments:
We are collecting videos of research activities to evaluate how well AI models can understand research intentions and goals in the lab!
Refer interested lab members to participate in:
1. Interview (30m) Orientation, instructions, and scheduling
2. Recording (2hr) Record two ~1-hour sessions in the lab
3. Reflection (30m) Interview about actions / intentions / goals in lab sessions
This research will produce a dataset of hands-on laboratory workflows for benchmarking AI models’ understanding of how actions connect to scientific goals and intentions.
Long-horizonLongitudinalDigital

ResearchWorkflow

Lead: Khanh Chi Le · 6–12 months

If you are running a long-term research project in digital tools: let us record how a project unfolds from inception to publication, to inform the next generation of AI research assistants.

  • CapturesKeystrokes, screen video, meeting notes, documents
  • MethodAuto-recording app + voluntary artifact submission
  • BecomesLongitudinal dataset of how research actually unfolds
  • CommitmentLight setup, then passive over the project

Contact: Khanh Chi Le ↗

If your lab works mostly in digital tools:
We are recording research projects from inception to publication to understand how research unfolds and inform AI research assistants!
Refer interested lab members to participate in:
1. Onboarding (1hr) Set up recording and connect your tools
2. Record & collect (12mo) Auto-records sessions and collects research files
3. Reflection (3 × 1hr) Interview about your research reasoning / decisions
This research will produce a dataset of longitudinal research workflows for studying how early actions shape later actions and outcomes, and building better AI research assistants.

How to get involved

Participate

Contribute your own scientific work — a couple of hours of lab recording, a set of computational tasks, or passive recording of a project you're already running. We handle setup, scheduling, and equipment.

For graduate students & lab members

Collaborate

Bring your lab into one or more of the projects. PIs help define what "correct" looks like in their domain and open the door for their students to participate.

For PIs & research groups across departments

Co-authorship and contribution opportunities are available for participating PIs and lab members across all three projects.

This initiative — and the team behind it — is featured in the Minnesota NLP Lab project portfolio, alongside our other work on expert workflows in science, law, education, and journalism.
